Some General Neuroscience Books:

1. Principles of Neural Science, by E.R. Kandel, J.H. Schwartz, and T.M. Jessel, McGraw-Hill, 2000

This text presents a general background in neuroscience, covering everything from genetics topics to developmental and behavioral aspects of neuroscience. It is also the only text that makes calculus books look small, but it has great graphics and pictures, and is well written.

2. Neurobiology,by G.M. Shepherd, Oxford Univ. Press, 1994

This is probably the only text that competes with the Kandel-Schwartz-Jessel text, again being very comprehensive and well written introduction (and a more manageable size).

Some Mathematical/Computational Neuroscience Books:

1. Theoretical Neuroscience, by P. Dayan and L.F. Abbott, MIT Press, 2001

This is a brand new text by outstanding researchers. I am enthusiastic about this book but I thought the amount of math preparation required would be more than our text, but there is a world of information in this book.

2. Introduction to Theoretical Neurobiology, vol. 1, 2, by H.C. Tuckwell, Cambridge Univ. Press, 1988

This text covers a lot of cable theory, including Rall's equivalent cylinder theory of dendritic branching, along with even stochastic modeling topics. I view it more as a graduate text, and there is not a lot of empirical studies discussed.

3. Biophysics of Computation, by C. Koch, Oxford Univ. Press, 1999

This book focuses on information processing in single neurons and is pretty comprehensive in that way. Some of the math is handled carelessly, but there is a lot of good topic here.

4. The Computational Brain, by P.S. Churchland and T.J. Sejnowski, MIT Press, 1992

This is a rather unique text as a collaboration between a computational neuroscientist and a behaviorial neurophysiologist. They try to link up neurobiological work with neural network paradigms in a rather conversational style, minimizing the math.

5. The Handbook of Brain Theory and Neural Networks, by M.A. Arbib (ed.), MIT Press, 1998

This is really a collection of short papers, but covering a hugh number of topics, and being well cross-referenced.

6. Electric Current Flow in Excitable Cells, by J.J.B. Jack, D. Noble, and R.W. Tsien, Clarendon Press, 1975

This is one of the early texts in the area, somewhat dated now, but has material found nowhere else.

7. Modeling in the Neurosciences, from ionic channels to neural networks, by R.R. Poznanski (ed.), Harwood Academic Press, 1999

8. Methods in Neuronal Modeling, from synapses to networks, by C. Koch and I. Segev (eds.), MIT Press, 1989

Both items 7 and 8 are collections of articles covering important topics in mathematical neurosciences.
