MATH 490/710 Dynamical Foundations of Neuroscience

Spring 2002

Mission: The course will offer an introduction to mathematical models and their analyses across an array of topics from theoretical neuroscience. Our goal is to see how quantitative models and simulations can extend our understanding of dynamic behavior of neurobiological observations, and provide tools for analyzing other situations.

Prerequisites: Calculus is essential, and some programming experience, differential equations and linear algebra background is desirable. Mathematically we begin with first order differential equations, but eventually analyze nonlinear systems representing activity of populations of excitable cells.

Course Requirements: There will be a number of homework assignments. Most of these are anticipated to be analytical in nature, but some will be interpretive, and some might be computational. I would like to assign a written project done toward the end of the course. Individual topics will be decided halfway through the semester and they will need to be completed before the end of the classes. Grade will be based on 80% for homework and 20% for the project. I do encourage students discuss homework and lectures out of class, particularly in an interdisciplinary course such as this, where math and biology students can play off their backgrounds. But assignments must be written up individually. Students registered for graduate credit can be expected to do extra work.

Text: Spikes, Decisions, and Actions, by Hugh R. Wilson, Oxford Press, 1999.

Support materials:I will put some material on reserve in the library under Math 490 reference. This may include books to read extra background from, and perhaps papers to get ideas from for the term projects.

Topics:This list is dynamic.. it is subject to change as the semester progresses

  1. Introduction to some neuroscience background: levels of organization, neuron parts and functions, principle variables; comments on behavior, neural networks, memory, etc.
  2. Simplest models involving first order dynamics: RC circuits and cell membrane models, models of excitatory post-synaptic potential, continuous firing rate models
  3. Classical Hodgkin-Huxley theory: experiments and modeling, discussion of time scales and reduction of the model
  4. Review of 2D linear systems, applications to feedback in retina, oscillating networks, muscle contraction
  5. Nonlinear 2D systems: steady states, geometric ideas, linearization,stability of steady states, applications to certain single nerve cell models, short-term memory circuits, introduction to hysteresis, adaptation, and competition
  6. Oscillations in single nerves and networks: limit cycles, P-B theory, applications to single nerve cell models and an oscillator network; use of Hopf bifurcation theory in explaining repetitive behavior in nerve models; various types of bursting behavior in nerves
  7. Modeling synapses and transmitter release; introduction of various forms of synaptic plasticity, Hebbian and anti-hebbian rules, activity-dependent plasticity, stability and competition, associative memory models
  8. Coupled neurons, phase oscillator models, and central pattern generators, inhibitory swimming networks, Lamprey and Tritonia swimming models
  9. Neural networks and neural computation: use of Lyapunov methods to analyze simple memory systems, some basics of some artificial neural networks; what can neuroscience say to engineering neural network algorithms. Here we may divert to recent studies on the neural ode and discuss pulsed neural networks and some model comparisons
